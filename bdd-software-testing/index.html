BDD

The one true testing revolution

Behaviour Driven Development and its close relative Acceptance Test Driven Development truly turn the testing world on its head. How so? Instead of testing after development, you test before. This seems simple but the ramifications are considerable.

Many testing techniques and ideas have been called revolutions but regrettably, the truth is that the majority are nothing more than a band-aid style solution and solve next to nothing with the testing process.

Why test before?
The point of testing beforehand is that the commitment made is much smaller (and cheaper) at the beginning rather than at the end at which point countless development hours have been wasted.

Product Defects

There are 2 types of defect that can affect your product

"

Implementation Defect

The product doesn't work as intended by the developers, either by crashing or by producing the wrong results. These defects are normally due to bugs; perhaps the developers made a mistake or made an incorrect assumption as to how something works.

B

Requirements Defect

The product works exactly as intended by the developers, but the users or customers are not satisfied with it. These are issues which have nothing to do with bad or sloppy code, rather with lack of clarity and miscommunication in the requirements capture process. Unstated user expectations and tacit, misunderstood or simply incorrect requirements are typical causes of Requirements Defects.

The Avoidable Cost

Anything up to 50-60% of issues identified by software testers can be chalked down as Requirements Defects. Unfortunately, because the code has been written, defects such as these cost between 100% to 200% more to fix than other defects.

Why are testers looking for defects after the implementation work has been carried out? If 50-60% of all the issues identified by software testers are Requirements Defects, why have they been left in for developers to work on? This is a massive wasted effort, not to mention waste of time and budget.

The Revolution Is

Behaviour Driven Development

Now you might say "But this doesn't apply to me and my team because we work Agile, so how would BBD help in my case?" While Agile truly does help to minimize instances of massive do-overs, in truth, all to often what Agile does is mitigate by finding errors and bugs after two weeks instead of after two months. However, BDD irons all of these issues out before they are ever coded, during the requirements phase so that the vast majority of defects quite simply never come into being.

So, how does BBD work then?

Behaviour Driven Development (and in turn our very own product Behave for JIRA) work like so. The business analyst or specialist liaises with requirements manager and tester and together they test out requirements between them, using scenarios. This collaboration uncovers where issues and defects are likely to happen much faster and more efficiently than any other approach. If you use user stories as the basis of requirements, the user stories get fleshed out and detailed in this way. In the briefest and simplest of terms, BDD works simply by describing what you want.

After all, and everyone knows this, the better the requirements are the better the quality of the work and the better the quality of the software.

What does BDD change?

BDD does change a few things, but worry not, we're not reinventing the wheel here, merely optimising it.

  • BDD brings a change in the tester's role since some of their time moves to the front of the chain when they test the requirements, instead of the same time being expended at the end of the chain when testing the code.
  • And of course BDD changes software development by making it much more focused on and grounded in quality, tested and proven requirements.

But isn't BDD and the like all about Test Automation?

Wrong. It's true that BDD and ATDD tools such as Cucumber and SpecFlow are often used in Test Automation however, that is only the tip of the proverbial iceberg. Saying that these tools are simply Test Automation tools is a rather narrow viewpoint which completely misses the benefits brought about by the hugely improved requirements and time saved as well as the cost savings brought about by these changes in workflow and methodology.
